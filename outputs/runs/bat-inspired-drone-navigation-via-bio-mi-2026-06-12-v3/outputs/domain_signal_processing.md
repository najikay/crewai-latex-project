# Chapter 4–5: Bio-Inspired SLAM and Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion — An Aerospace and Marine Navigation Perspective

## 4.1 Problem Formulation: The Unified State Space

The navigation problem for a bat-inspired drone operating in a GPS-denied, confined environment is formally identical to that of a submarine or AUV navigating a deep-sea canyon or an Arctic under-ice corridor. In both domains, the vehicle must estimate its six-degree-of-freedom (6-DOF) pose — position $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and attitude represented by a unit quaternion $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{S}^3$ — along with its linear velocity $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, IMU sensor biases $\mathbf{b}_g, \mathbf{b}_a \in \mathbb{R}^3$, and the positions of $N$ environmental landmarks $\mathbf{m}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ \cite{Thrun2005, Bailey2006}. The full state vector at time step $k$ is:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_k = \left[ \mathbf{p}_k^{\mathsf{T}},\; \mathbf{v}_k^{\mathsf{T}},\; \mathbf{q}_k^{\mathsf{T}},\; \mathbf{b}_{g,k}^{\mathsf{T}},\; \mathbf{b}_{a,k}^{\mathsf{T}},\; \mathbf{m}_{1,k}^{\mathsf{T}},\; \ldots,\; \mathbf{m}_{N,k}^{\mathsf{T}} \right]^{\mathsf{T}} \in \mathbb{R}^{15 + 3N}
\label{eq:state_vector}
\end{equation}

This formulation is directly analogous to the state vector used in submarine inertial navigation systems (INS) when fused with Doppler velocity log (DVL) and acoustic positioning (USBL/LBL) \cite{Fossen2011}. The key difference — and the bio-inspired innovation — is that the acoustic observations are generated by an active sonar system mimicking bat CF-FM echolocation rather than a conventional DVL or USBL transponder network.

**Assumption 1 (Rigid-Body Dynamics):** The drone is modeled as a rigid body with constant mass and inertia tensor. Small-angle approximations are avoided; attitude is propagated using quaternion kinematics to prevent gimbal lock during aggressive maneuvers in confined spaces \cite{Trawny2005}.

**Assumption 2 (Confined-Space Acoustics):** The environment is characterized by specular wall reflections and multi-path propagation, identical to the acoustic conditions in submarine navigation through fjords and underwater canyons \cite{Jensen2011}. The LFM chirp used by the bat-inspired sonar and the active sonar pulse of a submarine are processed by the same matched filter for the same purpose: extracting range from a reverberant enclosed space.

## 4.2 Motion Model: UAV Dynamics with Quaternion Kinematics

The discrete-time motion model propagates the state from time $k$ to $k+1$ using IMU measurements $\mathbf{u}_k = [\boldsymbol{\omega}_k^{\mathsf{T}}, \mathbf{a}_k^{\mathsf{T}}]^{\mathsf{T}}$, where $\boldsymbol{\omega}_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the angular velocity and $\mathbf{a}_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the specific force, both expressed in the body frame \cite{Maybeck1979}:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{u}_k, \mathbf{w}_k)
\label{eq:motion_model}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{w}_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{Q}_k)$ is the process noise. The specific components of $\mathbf{f}$ are:

**Position update:**
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}_{k+1} = \mathbf{p}_k + \mathbf{v}_k \Delta t + \frac{1}{2} \left( \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}_k) \mathbf{a}_k + \mathbf{g} \right) \Delta t^2
\label{eq:position_update}
\end{equation}

**Velocity update:**
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{v}_{k+1} = \mathbf{v}_k + \left( \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}_k) \mathbf{a}_k + \mathbf{g} \right) \Delta t
\label{eq:velocity_update}
\end{equation}

**Quaternion kinematics (attitude update):**
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{q}_{k+1} = \mathbf{q}_k \otimes \exp\left( \frac{\Delta t}{2} \boldsymbol{\omega}_k \right)
\label{eq:quat_kinematics}
\end{equation}

where $\otimes$ denotes the Hamilton quaternion product and $\exp(\cdot)$ maps a rotation vector to a unit quaternion \cite{Trawny2005}. The rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}) \in SO(3)$ is computed from the quaternion as:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{I}_3 + 2q_0 [\mathbf{q}_{1:3}]_{\times} + 2 [\mathbf{q}_{1:3}]_{\times}^2
\label{eq:rotation_matrix}
\end{equation}

where $[\cdot]_{\times}$ denotes the skew-symmetric cross-product matrix.

**IMU bias evolution (random walk model):**
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{b}_{g,k+1} = \mathbf{b}_{g,k} + \mathbf{n}_{bg}, \quad \mathbf{b}_{a,k+1} = \mathbf{b}_{a,k} + \mathbf{n}_{ba}
\label{eq:bias_model}
\end{equation}

with $\mathbf{n}_{bg} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{bg}^2 \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t)$ and $\mathbf{n}_{ba} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{ba}^2 \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t)$.

**Landmark stationarity assumption:**
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{m}_{i,k+1} = \mathbf{m}_{i,k} \quad \forall i
\label{eq:landmark_static}
\end{equation}

This motion model is structurally identical to the strapdown INS algorithm used in submarine navigation, where the IMU provides high-rate (100–200 Hz) angular rate and specific force measurements that are integrated to propagate position, velocity, and attitude \cite{Titterton2004}. The key difference is that the submarine's DVL provides bottom-track velocity measurements at 1–10 Hz to bound the INS drift, whereas the bat-inspired drone uses sonar range and Doppler observations for the same purpose.

### 4.2.1 Jacobian of the Motion Model

The EKF requires the Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_k = \partial \mathbf{f} / \partial \mathbf{x} |_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k}, \mathbf{u}_k}$ for covariance propagation. The non-zero blocks of $\mathbf{F}_k$ are:

\begin{align}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{p}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{p}_k} &= \mathbf{I}_3, \quad 
\frac{\partial \mathbf{p}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_k} = \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t, \quad 
\frac{\partial \mathbf{p}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{q}_k} = -\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}_k) [\mathbf{a}_k]_{\times} \Delta t^2 \nonumber \\
\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_k} &= \mathbf{I}_3, \quad 
\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{q}_k} = -\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{q}_k) [\mathbf{a}_k]_{\times} \Delta t \nonumber \\
\frac{\partial \mathbf{q}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{q}_k} &= \exp\left( \frac{\Delta t}{2} \boldsymbol{\omega}_k \right), \quad 
\frac{\partial \mathbf{q}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{b}_{g,k}} = -\mathbf{q}_k \otimes \frac{\Delta t}{2} \mathbf{I}_3 \nonumber \\
\frac{\partial \mathbf{b}_{g,k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{b}_{g,k}} &= \mathbf{I}_3, \quad 
\frac{\partial \mathbf{b}_{a,k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{b}_{a,k}} = \mathbf{I}_3
\label{eq:jacobian_F}
\end{align}

The full Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{F}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{(15+3N) \times (15+3N)}$ is block-diagonal in the landmark sub-block (identity) and upper-triangular in the vehicle sub-block.

## 4.3 Observation Model: Bat-Inspired Echolocation with Submarine Parallels

The observation model maps the state to sonar measurements. The bat-inspired sonar system transmits a CF-FM chirp and receives echoes from environmental landmarks. Three types of observations are extracted: range $z_{r,i}$, Doppler velocity $z_{d,i}$, and bearing $\theta_i$ (from interaural time difference, ITD).

### 4.3.1 Range Observation

The range to landmark $i$ is the Euclidean distance plus Gaussian noise \cite{Thrun2005}:

\begin{equation}
z_{r,i} = \| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \| + n_r, \quad n_r \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_r^2)
\label{eq:range_obs}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_r^2$ follows the uncertainty model \cite{Velmurugan2026}:

\begin{equation}
\sigma_r^2(z) = \sigma_0^2 + \sigma_1^2 \cdot z^2
\label{eq:range_uncertainty}
\end{equation}

with $\sigma_0 = 0.001$ m and $\sigma_1 = 0.01$ (dimensionless) for the Murata MA40S4S transducer at 40 kHz \cite{Murata2022}.

**Submarine parallel:** This is functionally identical to the two-way travel time ranging used in submarine active sonar and LBL acoustic positioning systems \cite{Jensen2011}. The matched filter processing of the LFM chirp — whether emitted by a bat, a drone sonar, or a submarine — yields the same range estimate via $\tau = 2R/c$, where $c$ is the speed of sound in the medium (343 m/s in air, ~1500 m/s in water).

### 4.3.2 Doppler Velocity Observation

The Doppler shift of the echo provides the relative radial velocity between the drone and the landmark \cite{Schnitzler2011}:

\begin{equation}
z_{d,i} = \frac{(\mathbf{v}_k - \dot{\mathbf{m}}_i)^{\mathsf{T}} (\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|} + n_d, \quad n_d \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_d^2)
\label{eq:doppler_obs}
\end{equation}

For stationary landmarks ($\dot{\mathbf{m}}_i = \mathbf{0}$), this simplifies to the projection of the drone's velocity onto the line-of-sight vector. The Doppler shift in Hz is:

\begin{equation}
f_d = -\frac{2 f_0}{c} \cdot z_{d,i}
\label{eq:doppler_hz}
\end{equation}

where $f_0 = 40$ kHz is the carrier frequency.

**Submarine parallel:** Submarines use Doppler shift from active sonar returns to estimate target radial velocity and to perform own-Doppler nullification (ODN) — the same mathematical operation as bat Doppler shift compensation (DSC) \cite{Schnitzler2011}. The control law for adjusting the transmit frequency to compensate for ego-motion is:

\begin{equation}
\dot{f}_c = K_p \cdot (f_{ref} - f_e) + K_i \int (f_{ref} - f_e) dt
\label{eq:dsc_control}
\end{equation}

where $f_c$ is the call frequency, $f_e$ is the echo frequency, and $f_{ref}$ is the reference frequency (the bat's "resting" frequency). In the drone implementation, $f_{ref}$ is replaced by the expected echo frequency for a stationary target, computed from the IMU-estimated velocity \cite{Muller2024BatDeck}:

\begin{equation}
\hat{f}_d = -\frac{2 f_0}{c} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{v}_k^{\mathsf{T}} (\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|}
\label{eq:est_doppler}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f_{tx} = f_0 + \hat{f}_d
\label{eq:tx_freq_adj}
\end{equation}

This closed-loop Doppler compensation is critical for maintaining echo lock in high-speed flight through tunnels, just as it is for a submarine tracking a fast-moving target in a multi-path-dominated canyon environment.

### 4.3.3 Bearing Observation from Interaural Time Difference

The bearing to landmark $i$ is estimated from the time difference of arrival (TDOA) between two microphones separated by distance $d$ \cite{Borina2011}:

\begin{equation}
\theta_i = \arcsin\left( \frac{c \cdot \Delta t_{ITD}}{d} \right) + n_\theta, \quad n_\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\theta^2)
\label{eq:itd_bearing}
\end{equation}

For the 4-element microphone array (Knowles SPU0410LR5H-QB) with $d = 20$ mm and a sampling rate of 500 kHz, the theoretical angular resolution is approximately $1.5^\circ$ at broadside \cite{Muller2024BatDeck}.

**Submarine parallel:** This is directly analogous to the phase-difference-based bearing estimation used in submarine towed array sonar and USBL positioning systems \cite{Jensen2011}. The delay-and-sum beamformer used in both domains is:

\begin{equation}
B(\theta, \phi) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} w_m \cdot r_m(t - \tau_m(\theta, \phi))
\label{eq:beamformer}
\end{equation}

where $w_m$ are array weights (e.g., Chebyshev for sidelobe suppression) and $\tau_m$ are the steering delays.

## 4.4 Extended Kalman Filter SLAM

The EKF-SLAM algorithm fuses the IMU predictions with sonar observations to produce a consistent estimate of the vehicle state and landmark positions \cite{Thrun2005, Davison2007}.

### 4.4.1 Prediction Step

The predicted state and covariance at time $k+1$ given measurements up to time $k$ are:

\begin{align}
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k+1|k} &= \mathbf{f}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k}, \mathbf{u}_k, \mathbf{0}) \label{eq:ekf_pred_state} \\
\mathbf{P}_{k+1|k} &= \mathbf{F}_k \mathbf{P}_{k|k} \mathbf{F}_k^{\mathsf{T}} + \mathbf{Q}_k \label{eq:ekf_pred_cov}
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{Q}_k$ is the process noise covariance matrix, which includes contributions from IMU noise (angle random walk, velocity random walk) and bias drift:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}_k = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{1}{4} \mathbf{R}_k [\mathbf{a}_k]_{\times} \sigma_{g,ARW}^2 \Delta t^3 [\mathbf{a}_k]_{\times}^{\mathsf{T}} \mathbf{R}_k^{\mathsf{T}} & \cdots \\
\vdots & \sigma_{a,VRW}^2 \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t & \cdots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \sigma_{bg}^2 \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t & \cdots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \sigma_{ba}^2 \mathbf{I}_3 \Delta t
\end{bmatrix}
\label{eq:Q_matrix}
\end{equation}

The IMU noise parameters, obtained from Allan variance analysis of the BMI270 \cite{Bosch2021}, are:

\begin{equation}
\sigma_{g,ARW} = 0.004 \, \text{rad/s/}\sqrt{\text{Hz}}, \quad \sigma_{a,VRW} = 0.08 \, \text{m/s}^2/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}, \quad \sigma_{bg} = 1.0 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{rad/s}^2/\text{Hz}, \quad \sigma_{ba} = 1.0 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{m/s}^3/\text{Hz}
\label{eq:imu_noise_params}
\end{equation}

### 4.4.2 Update Step

When a sonar observation $\mathbf{z}_{k+1} = [z_{r,i}, z_{d,i}, \theta_i]^{\mathsf{T}}$ is available, the EKF update is performed:

\begin{align}
\mathbf{K}_{k+1} &= \mathbf{P}_{k+1|k} \mathbf{H}_{k+1}^{\mathsf{T}} \left( \mathbf{H}_{k+1} \mathbf{P}_{k+1|k} \mathbf{H}_{k+1}^{\mathsf{T}} + \mathbf{R}_{k+1} \right)^{-1} \label{eq:ekf_gain} \\
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k+1|k+1} &= \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k+1|k} + \mathbf{K}_{k+1} \left( \mathbf{z}_{k+1} - \mathbf{h}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k+1|k}) \right) \label{eq:ekf_update_state} \\
\mathbf{P}_{k+1|k+1} &= \left( \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_{k+1} \mathbf{H}_{k+1} \right) \mathbf{P}_{k+1|k} \label{eq:ekf_update_cov}
\end{align}

The observation Jacobian $\mathbf{H}_{k+1} = \partial \mathbf{h} / \partial \mathbf{x} |_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k+1|k}}$ has non-zero blocks for the vehicle state and the observed landmark:

\begin{align}
\frac{\partial z_{r,i}}{\partial \mathbf{p}_k} &= \frac{(\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)^{\mathsf{T}}}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|}, \quad 
\frac{\partial z_{r,i}}{\partial \mathbf{m}_i} = -\frac{(\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)^{\mathsf{T}}}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|} \nonumber \\
\frac{\partial z_{d,i}}{\partial \mathbf{p}_k} &= \frac{\mathbf{v}_k^{\mathsf{T}}}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|} - \frac{(\mathbf{v}_k^{\mathsf{T}} (\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)) (\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)^{\mathsf{T}}}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|^3}, \quad 
\frac{\partial z_{d,i}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_k} = \frac{(\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i)^{\mathsf{T}}}{\| \mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{m}_i \|} \nonumber \\
\frac{\partial \theta_i}{\partial \mathbf{p}_k} &= \frac{c}{d \cos \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \Delta t_{ITD}}{\partial \mathbf{p}_k}
\label{eq:jacobian_H}
\end{align}

The observation noise covariance matrix is:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R}_{k+1} = \text{diag}\left( \sigma_r^2(z),\; \sigma_d^2,\; \sigma_\theta^2 \right)
\label{eq:R_matrix}
\end{equation}

with $\sigma_d = 0.05$ m/s and $\sigma_\theta = 0.026$ rad ($1.5^\circ$) \cite{Muller2024BatDeck}.

### 4.4.3 Data Association and Outlier Rejection

Data association between sonar observations and existing landmarks is performed using the Mahalanobis distance gating test \cite{Maybeck1979}:

\begin{equation}
\gamma_k = (\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{h}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1}))^{\mathsf{T}} \mathbf{S}_k^{-1} (\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{h}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1})) < \chi^2_{0.95, m}
\label{eq:chi_sq_gate}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{S}_k = \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^{\mathsf{T}} + \mathbf{R}_k$ is the innovation covariance and $m = 3$ is the observation dimension. Observations failing this test are either rejected as outliers or used to initialize new landmarks.

### 4.4.4 Landmark Initialization

A new landmark is initialized from a single sonar ping using the range and bearing estimates \cite{Davison2007}:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{m}_i = \mathbf{p}_k + z_{r,i} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta_i \cos\phi_i \\ \sin\theta_i \cos\phi_i \\ \sin\phi_i \end{bmatrix}
\label{eq:landmark_init}
\end{equation}

where $\phi_i$ is the elevation angle (estimated from the vertical microphone pair). The initial covariance of the new landmark is computed via linearized error propagation:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{P}_{mm,i} = \mathbf{J}_m \mathbf{P}_{k|k} \mathbf{J}_m^{\mathsf{T}} + \mathbf{J}_z \mathbf{R}_k \mathbf{J}_z^{\mathsf{T}}
\label{eq:landmark_cov}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{J}_m$ and $\mathbf{J}_z$ are the Jacobians of the initialization function with respect to the vehicle state and the observation, respectively.

## 4.5 Adaptive Noise Covariance Estimation

To handle the time-varying noise characteristics of the sonar system (e.g., propeller-induced noise, multi-path interference), an innovation-based adaptive estimation scheme is employed \cite{MohamedSchwarz1999}:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{R}}_k = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=k-N+1}^{k} \boldsymbol{\nu}_i \boldsymbol{\nu}_i^{\mathsf{T}} - \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^{\mathsf{T}}
\label{eq:adaptive_R}
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\nu}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{h}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{i|i-1})$ is the innovation at time $i$, and $N = 20$ is the window size (corresponding to 1 second of sonar data at 20 Hz). The adaptive estimate $\hat{\mathbf{R}}_k$ is constrained to be positive definite by projecting onto the cone of positive semidefinite matrices.

## 4.6 The Submarine–Drone Parallel: A Quantitative Comparison

The mathematical and algorithmic parallels between bat-inspired drone navigation in tunnels and submarine navigation in underwater canyons are summarized in Table 4.1.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Quantitative comparison of acoustic navigation in drones and submarines.}
\label{tab:submarine_drone_parallel}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Drone (Air)} & \textbf{Submarine (Water)} \\
\hline
Speed of sound, $c$ & 343 m/s & 1500 m/s \\
Carrier frequency, $f_0$ & 40 kHz & 10–100 kHz \\
Typical range & 0.5–8 m & 10–10,000 m \\
Doppler shift at 1 m/s & $\pm$233 Hz & $\pm$13.3 Hz \\
Multi-path delay spread & 0.1–10 ms & 1–100 ms \\

Matched filter gain & $BT$ (time-bandwidth product) & $BT$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The key insight — which forms the theoretical foundation of this work — is that the LFM chirp used by the bat-inspired sonar and the active sonar pulse of a submarine are the same signal, processed by the same matched filter, for the same reason: extracting range from a reverberant enclosed space \cite{Jensen2011, Schnitzler2011}. The multi-path environment in a tunnel (specular wall reflections, standing-wave interference) is physically identical to that in a fjord or underwater canyon. The mitigation strategies — MUSIC/ESPRIT super-resolution, time-gating, and spatial diversity — are common to both domains.

## 4.7 Algorithm Summary

The complete bio-inspired EKF-SLAM algorithm is presented in Algorithm 1.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Bio-Mimetic EKF-SLAM with Doppler Compensation}
\label{alg:ekf_slam}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require IMU measurements $\mathbf{u}_k = [\boldsymbol{\omega}_k, \mathbf{a}_k]$, sonar observations $\mathbf{z}_k = [z_{r,i}, z_{d,i}, \theta_i]$
\Ensure State estimate $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k}$, covariance $\mathbf{P}_{k|k}$
\State Initialize $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0$, $\mathbf{P}_0$, $\mathbf{Q}_0$, $\mathbf{R}_0$
\For{$k = 1$ to $N$}
    \State \textbf{Prediction:} Propagate state via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:position_update}--\eqref{eq:bias_model}
    \State Compute Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_k$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:jacobian_F}
    \State Propagate covariance via Eq.~\eqref{eq:ekf_pred_cov}
    \State \textbf{Doppler Compensation:} Compute $\hat{f}_d$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:est_doppler}
    \State Adjust transmit frequency: $f_{tx} = f_0 + \hat{f}_d$
    \State \textbf{Observation:} Receive sonar echoes
    \For{each detected landmark $i$}
        \State Compute predicted observation $\mathbf{h}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k|k-1})$
        \State Compute innovation $\boldsymbol{\nu}_i$ and covariance $\mathbf{S}_i$
        \If{$\boldsymbol{\nu}_i^{\mathsf{T}} \mathbf{S}_i^{-1} \boldsymbol{\nu}_i < \chi^2_{0.95, 3}$}
            \State Update state and covariance via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ekf_gain}--\eqref{eq:ekf_update_cov}
        \Else
            \State Initialize new landmark via Eq.~\eqref{eq:landmark_init}
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textbf{Adaptive Noise:} Update $\hat{\mathbf{R}}_k$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:adaptive_R}
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

## 4.8 Conclusion

This chapter has presented the complete mathematical framework for bio-inspired SLAM and multi-modal sensor fusion, drawing explicit parallels between bat echolocation, drone navigation in tunnels, and submarine navigation in underwater canyons. The 6-DOF motion model with quaternion kinematics, the bat-inspired observation model with Doppler compensation, and the EKF-SLAM formulation with adaptive noise covariance together form the algorithmic core of the proposed system. The submarine–drone parallel is not merely analogical but mathematical: the same matched-filter processing, the same multi-path mitigation strategies, and the same sensor fusion architectures apply across both domains, unified by the physics of acoustic wave propagation in confined spaces.
